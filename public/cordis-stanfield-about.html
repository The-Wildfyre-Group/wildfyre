NEVER WILL / SACRIFICE THE GIFT / NAKED / WILDFYRE

Financial engineer, software engineer, and lead consultant for early-stage companies. Leverage unique skill-set of financial and technical expertise to advise on a wide range of needs including foundational funding decisions, business model creation, and database design.



“If you don’t do it now, you never will.”


These were the words spoken by my first finance professor and later turned mentor, Pamela Turner, CFP, CFA, Phd, as I sat in the front of Finance Principles 101. The 'it' she was referring to was the process of becoming a Wall Street investment banking analyst. The previous weekend, I’d scoured the pages of a paperback book aimed at demystifying the investment banking experience. The book served it’s purpose; my eyes were widened, but not in the way my professor had hoped. In fact, I was thoroughly put off by the tiring hours, expensive rents, ostensibly-permeating pretentiousness, and otherwise negative narrative; however, the gaudy bonuses somehow blanketed all of the minuses in one fell swoop.


“To give anything less than your best, is to sacrifice the gift”


As a collegiate distance runner, Steve Prefontaine’s glorified quote was fitting for my situation. Pamela reiterated her stance: I was the most qualified of her students and there was an opportunity. In a classic case study for human behavioral influences, in some twisted way, I somehow became singularly interested in banking. The fact that I felt like I could be a frontrunner in a race that apparently everyone (else) wanted to win made me want to compete in said race. I further strengthened my candidacy, got hired by Morgan Stanley, and successfully completed the two-year analyst program.


“Naked Business Card”


I left MS with enough skill to hit the ground running in any organization. I relocated from NYC to DC to join Xavier Capital, a firm specializing in fixed income portfolios co-founded by Pamela Turner, CFP, CFA, Phd. & Lorenzo Newsome, Jr. PRM, FrM, CFA (if you’re counting, that’s 9 letters after each name). Xavier Capital was only a three-person company, but it was seemingly teeming with CFAs. Everyone but me had their designation, so naturally, I had to get 'it' as well because all of the cool kids were doing it. I studied in solitude. I passed CFA Level 3 at age 26 and was finally able to add some letters to my name. I no longer had a naked business card.



W-W-W-WILDFYYYRRE


I’ve always felt that my personality and my interests have been consistent over time. I generally, not by design, like and pursue feats that are difficult and not always popularly pursued by the general population with vigor. Finance, web development, and entrepreneurship are all viewed somewhat similarly: arduous, technical, too demanding, and to some, too risky. Combine those with my hobby of choice, long distance running, and you have a recipe for a guy that prides himself on doing things other people can’t, won’t, and don’t do.

Although I pride myself on being technically capable, I feel that is more of a safety net - possessing a tangible skillset. Inwardly, I feel my talent as an innovator, thinker, and motivator rivals or evens exceeds my ability to get things done. This realization, or some unfounded irrational hubris, lead to the belief that Khadijah and I could combine our technical and innovative qualities, with our business backgrounds, to develop a collective of companies that spread like “Wildfyre.”
